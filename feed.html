curated feeds

You went out with your friends today and took plenty of pictures, especially a lot of staged candid photos. When you get back home, you go to post on your Instagram. You spend two hours choosing a picture, edit the lighting, add a filter, add grain so it lessens the ability to see the pimple on your cheek, spend another hour thinking of a funny and creative caption and send it to your friends to approve before posting. Then you assemble a group of actual candid moments and raw photos from the day, write out whatever caption that comes to mind and you post it on your finsta account without hesitation.

As we construct the ideal profile of ourselves, we’re aware that we are responsible for creating a complete image of ourselves which consists of the feeds and timelines we manage on social media. On platforms such as Tinder and Bumble, it’s almost normal to create fake profiles. People are well aware that many of the profiles are essentially nonsense. People alter their images as much as they possibly can in order to appeal to others. They’re consciously constructing a desirable profile and excluding anything they don’t like about themselves. This is the case on other platforms like Facebook and Instagram as well. People post and share images and updates that they feel seem to enhance the appearance of their profile. The platform may be different, but the intent is basically the same, to display an enticing profile to others and grab their attention. .

the impact

People fail to remember their past perfectly as they tend to only capture the happy moments and post those only. This further interferes with our own perception of our memories. We are convinced because we seem to be smiling and enjoying life in the pictures on our Instagram feeds or our Facebook memories. Elissa Gabbert discusses this idea of perception of ourselves in her essay, “Picture Yourself Happy”. She says taking photographs of herself allows for her to “confirm to myself that I am who I think I am.” However, as this image gets tampered with in the process of perfecting it to share with others, it loses the original value and becomes almost like a second, different, version of what actually happened. The image of ourselves we once wanted others to believe ends up convincing us and altering the truth of our past in a sense.
